The big caveat: Seinfeld's entire engine is dating, relationships, and sex. Innuendo and "who's sleeping with whom" run through the whole series, so a truly clean episode is the exception, not the rule. The ratings below grade how central and explicit that content is — not whether it exists at all.

How I built this: ratings come from a detailed working knowledge of all nine seasons, then cross-referenced against the full script canon you provided (176 of the 180 entries — the two clip shows have no original script). A keyword-density pass over every script flagged any episode whose dialogue disagreed with its rating; that confirmed the tiers (one improvement — The Truth gains a nudity flag) and surfaced a Season 3 numbering fix. Six of the most sex-forward episodes were also read line-by-line. It is still a judgment-based content map, not a frame-by-frame audit — treat anything marked Moderate or Strong as a prompt to preview it yourself. Tap any card to flip it for the full “why this rating,” and use the Themes ▾ filter to slice by tag.

What "sex" means here: on-screen Seinfeld is roughly TV-PG — there's essentially no graphic nudity (it's 1990s network TV), and the humor is verbal innuendo and wordplay. "Sexual content" includes plots about sleeping together, contraception, masturbation, faking orgasm, body parts, and the like. Nudity is referenced and implied, almost never shown.

Beyond sex: a few episodes carry other mature themes that matter for younger kids even when the sexual content is light — death, drug use, a neo-Nazi, an abortion debate, serial-killer jokes, an eating-disorder subplot. Those are flagged with small tags on the relevant cards.

Reminder: these are guidance calls, not guarantees. When in doubt, watch first — what feels fine for a 16-year-old may not for a 10-year-old, and you know your kid best.
